COLLECTION OF
BARON MÓR LIPÓT HERZOG (1869-1934)

A Jewish Art Collector in the Pre-War Hungary

Baron Mór Lipót Herzog's collection, at its zenith, comprised about 2500 artworks, displayed in the Andrássy Avenue palace of the Andrássy family. The private collection, showcasing world-renowned masters, included masterpieces by El Greco, Lucas Cranach, Corot, and Renoir, among others. Mór Lipót Herzog began dealing with artworks under the influence of another famous art collector-businessman of the era, Marcell Nemes (1866–1930). The wealth of the Herzog family, largely derived from tobacco production, allowed them to acquire not only popular works but also unconventional pieces on the international art market. Mór Lipót Herzog later purchased valuable pieces from Nemes's collection, including the now globally famous paintings by El Greco.

The reputation and recognition of the collection gradually grew in the 1920s, leading to the creation of an illustrated catalogue featuring the finest pieces in 1927. The expansion of the collection came to a halt with the death of Mór Lipót Herzog in 1934. The artworks were inherited by his wife, Janka Hatvany-Deutsch, and their three children: Erzsébet Herzog, András Herzog, and István Herzog. By the end of World War II, the collection had been dispersed. Some parts still adorn Hungarian museums and other public institutions. Another group of artworks was taken to the Soviet Union at that time, and some pieces have yet to resurface. Remaining artworks are found in Germany, Poland, and lost works occasionally reappear in international and domestic auctions. The heirs of Herzog tried for many years to recover art looted from their antecendent. In 2010, his heirs sued the Hungarian government for the return of more than 40 paintings seized during World War II, valued in excess of US$ 100 million.

Although the collection contained several handicrafts and pieces of furniture, only a selection of paintings by international artists is presented here, that are depicted in the publication: Elveszett örökség (Lost Heritage), edited by Péter Molnos, Kieselbach Galéria, Budapest, 2017, ISBN: 978-615-5199-13-4. This website aims to show different approaches to presenting a private collection: providing a description of the collector and the collection, presenting the collection in an image gallery, showing an overview of the dataset with data visualizations, localizing the artworks on a map, giving information on provenance with a timeline, diving deeper into the history of one art piece with a storytelling approach, and presenting the lost artworks in a grid gallery.

STORYLINE

A survivor masterpiece


Paul Gauguin's "Still Life with the Profile of Laval" is the artwork with the most detailed provenance and had the most owner over the 20th century. It's exiting journey represents the turbulent political situation in Hungary and makes the masterpiece a real survivor.
